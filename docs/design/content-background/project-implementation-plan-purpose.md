## Core Services and Expertise|BioCommons

The Australian BioCommons is leading the Core Services and Expertise Platform (CSEP), which represents a critical feature of the overall GUARDIANS program of work. The CSEP encompasses Projects 1A (Leadership and coordination), 1B (Technical expertise staff) and 1C (Supporting infrastructures) as depicted in the GUARDIANS Framework and underpins and actively contributes to all activities and projects within GUARDIANS.  The CSEP aims to build a skilled technical workforce, deliver core infrastructure for national projects, develop leadership services aligned with NAGIM principles, establish and operate foundational infrastructure services, and coordinate training and community development. These efforts will ensure sustainable, scalable, and cutting-edge national genomics infrastructure that support impactful research and innovation.  The BioCommons, as the lead coordinating and administering agency, has already made significant progress in establishing the CSEP in the first half of 2024 with the recruitment of several technical and managerial roles.

## A National Repository For Human Omics Research Data Connected To Computing Resources|NCI

NCI’s Genomics and Biomedical Data (GBD) Pillar aspires to provide Australian researchers and health service providers with a transformative infrastructure capability to solve their most pressing problems in data and computation. The GBD Pillar recognises that this transformative capability is not limited to data storage and a large bare-metal HPC cluster, but also utilising NCI’s High Performance Computing and Data infrastructure adjacent to its on-premise cloud for the deployment of foundational resources for Australia’s genomics community. A core resource will be building a nation-first secure, centralised repository for human genomic data that requires controlled access, and will evolve into Australia’s National Genome Repository. This repository will enable patient cohort owners, researchers, and the industry sector to host, access, and analyse human genomics cohorts that complies with the wishes of the consented patient participants, all in a securely managed environment. 
The vision for the repository is to achieve a sustainable model for housing Australia’s human genomics cohorts that enhances the standing of Australian researchers in their ability to collaborate, participate and lead international health-related studies. The success and centrality of the resource will ultimately create an incentive for funding agencies to mandate the housing of their funded cohorts in the repository. Access and usage of the repository will also be tracked, and regular reports will be generated for funding agencies to understand the impact of their funded projects. This is in contrast to the current ad hoc situation, where researchers are generally left to cobble together the required infrastructure and software components needed to take on these types of projects, and where funders can only rely on progress reports from successful applicants to better understand the impact of their funding.

## ZERO Data Commons: an interoperable human genome data sharing portal|CCI/ZERO

The Zero Childhood Cancer program is one of the world’s most advanced real-time precision medicine programs for children, adolescents and young adults (CAYA) with cancer. By 2025, ZERO will have linked clinical, molecular (including whole genome), and outcome data from 3000 CAYA patients with cancer. Where possible, genome data from their parents may also be included, with feasibility to be assessed during the data collection and integration activities. Sharing these data widely is essential for driving discovery research yet it remains difficult to do so while protecting patient privacy and adhering to patient consent for secondary data use. In this project, we will develop a human genome data commons that is standards-compliant where possible, while allowing for bespoke components where required. It will be based on findability, accessibility, interoperability, and reusability (FAIR) principles, that enables specific data to be rapidly discovered, requested, and shared. The metadata for these data will be sophisticated, allowing relevant data to be found using a combination of molecular and phenotypic queries (e.g. patients with a certain mutation, age, diagnosis and outcome). The phenotypic queries will be interoperable through extensive harmonisation with ontologies for diagnosis, anatomical location, measurements and more. This data harmonisation will be demonstrated through integration of these data with other national programs, and international paediatric cancer collaborators (e.g., CHOP, St Jude, Heidelberg). The ZERO Data Commons project is a flagship project under the GUARDIANS initiative and aims to:
● Leverage ZERO data (clinical, molecular, outcome and, if and where possible parent genome data) from 3000 patients to drive discovery research. 
● Enable data sharing while protecting data privacy and complying with patient consent for data use.  

## A data commons for integrated human multi-omics data|UniMelb — PX4/OMIX3

The OMIX3 project (2024-27 inclusive) aims to establish a high-capacity, integrated multi-omics platform that provides end-to-end solutions for the collection, analysis, and secure storage of multi-omic data from clinical samples. It is being led and administered by the Melbourne School of Psychological Sciences, where the project’s Principal Investigator (PI), Prof Olivia Carter, is situated.  The collaboration aims to develop and implement a robust and secure data platform that supports the sophisticated needs of multi-omics research. BioCommons is the technical delivery partner on this data platform component, which includes ensuring data integrity, facilitating efficient data sharing and interoperability, and deploying advanced computational tools for data analysis. The objective of the project described in this document is to establish a national data platform for clinically accredited multi-omics data, which will integrate and facilitate the secure storage, sharing, and analysis of large-scale multi-omics datasets. This data platform exists within the broader OMIX3 project (OMIX3: high-capacity integrated multi-omics) funded by the MRFF with co-investment from partners including Thermo Fisher Scientific, Bioplatforms Australia, Australian BioCommons and the University of Melbourne. The work described in this document specifically concerns the establishment of a data platform for OMIX3, and while the interconnectedness of all parts of OMIX3 are acknowledged, this document does not aim to describe the full set of OMIX3 activities.


## Genomic Data Commons|QIMR Berghofer 

QIMRB is holding a very significant store of genomics data including whole genome sequence data from matched tumour/normal cancer samples, and the datasets from all the Australian projects within the International Cancer Genome Consortium (ICGC). The ICGC datasets includes whole genome sequencing for:
● 120 Ovarian cancer patients
● 250 Pancreatic cancer patients
● 600 Melanoma cancer patients
Additionally, QIMRB is holding substantial whole genome and RNA sequencing from studies conducted in oesophageal, lung and breast cancer, as well as mesothelioma.  QIMRB is also creating long read sequencing data and other genomics data including single cell qimr milestone 3 review and spatial transcriptomics. QIMRB will include as much of this additional data as consent and researcher desire permits.
Current practice for sharing genomic datasets is to send them to the European Genome/Phenome Archive (EGA) in England which is a free global service that holds and shares genomic data. Transferring data that is hundreds of Terabytes to Europe can take many months and, in our experience, has taken over half a year in the past. The transfer also frequently fails and needs to be restarted. 
The same problems occur when an Australian researcher tries to download data from EGA so while we endure the delays and difficulties once - at upload - every Australian researcher that tries to access our data encounters the same problems at download. The transfer problems slow and in some cases may block Australian researchers accessing genomic data created in Australia. This project will establish an Australian-based genomic data repository and document the process so other Australian labs looking to replicate our repository have a recipe to follow.
● Create a genomic repository in Australia for sharing QIMRB genomic datasets more easily with Australian researchers thereby increasing the accessibility and reusability of Australian omics data thereby increasing the accessibility and reusability of Australian omics data
● Document the architecture, design and implementation process to enable other Australian research groups with genomic datasets to replicate the repository.
● Integrate with federated trust and identity solutions and align with national standards for data sharing to enable more and better Australian genomic research through easier data sharing.

Rare genetic diseases are individually rare and yet collectively common impacting 8% (2 million) Australians. Diagnostic rates for rare disease have gradually increased to ~50% through application of genomic sequencing. However, this still leaves half of patients without a diagnosis. 

● Centralising and standardising access to currently disparate rare disease repositories, could improve diagnostic rates as researchers can draw from a large pool of data. 
● Deliver a user-centric platform that enables a dynamic, scalable, and ethical approach to data consent, management, and governance, 
● Centralising access to rare disease cohort data by creating an open, connected, and scalable genomics infrastructure that hosts multiple rare disease datasets,
● Demonstrate the value of a connected ecosystem by supporting the exploration of new research questions, by simplifying the process of sharing and reusing data.  

## Genomic Data Nodes|UniMelb — CCGCM

The objective of the project is to pilot components for a framework to manage genomic data within a multi-node environment, aligning components with the National Approach to Genomic Information Management (NAGIM) guidelines published by Australian Genomics.
In response to the limitations of a single centralised genomic database, the project proposes a model of “mutually trusted research environments,” where data can be shared and analysed across a network of Genomic Data Nodes (GDNs), all sharing common services and governance. This approach addresses challenges related to data access, security, and analytical complexity by providing a flexible, open-standards-based framework for managing genomic data.
The project brings together other GUARDIANS stakeholders and partners, with key contributions from Australian Genomics, BioCommons, Garvan, and AAF. It focuses on several key objectives: developing infrastructure for data submission, management, and access; ensuring comprehensive planning and documentation; integrating applications with the GDN system; and establishing robust reporting and control mechanisms. 
● Establish a reference implementation for GDNs
● Create and test core infrastructure components
● Ensure federated data exchange and interoperability
● Develop comprehensive documentation and user guidelines
● Implement reporting and control mechanisms
● Develop tools and interfaces for monitoring and managing data access, storage, and costs. This includes reporting and auditing functionality. Conduct user testing and integration proofs
● Ensure security and compliance
