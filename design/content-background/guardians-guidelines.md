# **GUARDIANS Guidelines**

The human Genomics Uplift for Australia through Research Data Infrastructure At National Scale (GUARDIANS) program represents a strategic national investment aimed at establishing world-class digital infrastructure to accelerate human genomics and related omics research in Australia.

## Preamble

Driven by major national initiatives like the Medical Research Future Fund (MRFF) missions, Australia is experiencing unprecedented growth in research programs underpinned by cutting-edge omics technologies. These technologies generate vast amounts of complex data, offering unprecedented opportunities to advance our understanding of human health, disease mechanisms, and precision medicine approaches.

However, the current landscape of human omics data sharing and analysis in Australia faces significant challenges. Existing solutions are often project-specific or institution-centric, lacking coordination, interoperability, and scalability to meet the rapidly growing demands. As highlighted by Australian Genomics, "Australia is facing a crisis in genomic information storage, analysis and sharing, and is at least five years behind other genomic national initiatives in coordinated approaches to health data management."

Funded through the National Collaborative Research Infrastructure Strategy (NCRIS), the four-year GUARDIANS program aims to address these challenges by delivering a coordinated national ecosystem of infrastructure, expertise, and services. This ecosystem will provide Australian researchers with seamless access to state-of-the-art tools, resources, and computational capabilities, enabling them to leverage the full potential of human omics data for groundbreaking discoveries and translational applications.

This document is intended to lay out a set of guidelines that will guide decision making in priority activities and projects for implementation in phase 1 of GUARDIANS (the first half of the program). It is important to note that the guidelines outlined below are not presented in order of importance and do not have scores or weightings attached to them. There is an expectation, however, that partners observe all guidelines and demonstrate that their proposal/s align with each guideline.

## Guidelines for Project Partners

1. **Scope:** GUARDIANS infrastructure platforms and components will support and uplift Australian human omics research communities across diverse domains, including genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and related multi-omics approaches. While the primary focus is on enabling cutting-edge research, the infrastructure should also be designed in such a way as to enable future clinical applications and commercial translation of human omics data, fostering a vibrant ecosystem for innovation.

2. **Need and Impact:** Infrastructure development within GUARDIANS must directly address well-defined unmet needs aligned with national health and medical research priorities, such as those outlined in the MRFF missions and other relevant strategic research initiatives. Expected impacts should be clearly articulated, such as:
   1. enabling transformative research insights,
   2. informing evidence-based clinical practices,
   3. driving industry innovation, or
   4. addressing specific challenges in human health and disease prevention, diagnosis, or treatment.

3. **National Focus:** All GUARDIANS activities must observe a national focus, serving research communities across Australia, while aligning with and contributing to international best practices and collaborations. The program will foster a coordinated national approach to human omics data management, analysis, and sharing. This coordinated approach will leverage Australian expertise and resources to establish a globally competitive research infrastructure ecosystem.

4. **Sustainability and Feasibility:** GUARDIANS infrastructure must be designed for scalable, long-term use and be feasible within the available resources and funding models. It should prioritise strategic integration and leverage existing infrastructure components, data resources, and expertise, maximising efficiency and cost-effectiveness. Sustainable operating models should be established to ensure continuity and growth beyond the initial funding period.

5. **Interoperability and Openness:** Interoperability is a fundamental principle for GUARDIANS infrastructure, ensuring seamless integration within the national ecosystem and alignment with international standards and best practices. Adherence to established data and metadata standards, such as those defined by the Global Alliance for Genomics and Health (GA4GH) and the FAIR data principles, will be essential. Open and transparent data sharing policies and practices will be promoted to facilitate interoperability, reuse of data and resources, and collaboration across institutions and borders.

6. **Coinvestment:** GUARDIANS infrastructure development will be driven by meaningful and substantive coinvestment from partner organisations in the form of financial contributions, measurable, dedicated in-kind support, and leveraging of significant resources. Coinvestment should align with partner priorities and strategic objectives, fostering a shared commitment to the program's success and enabling the long-term sustainability of the resulting infrastructure components.

7. **Governance:** GUARDIANS infrastructure will be subject to robust governance structures that ensure secure, legal, and ethical use of human omics data for consented research purposes. These governance frameworks will meet custodian obligations, enable research communities to achieve their goals, and promote world's best practices through appropriate international representation. Governance bodies will include diverse stakeholders, such as researchers, clinicians, ethicists, and community representatives, to ensure a comprehensive and inclusive approach.

8. **Innovation:** A core objective of GUARDIANS is to drive greater innovation in the human omics research community and the Australian research sector more broadly. Infrastructure components will be designed to enable novel analysis methods, facilitate the integration of diverse omics data types. By providing cutting-edge capabilities, GUARDIANS will catalyse new insights, applications, and translational opportunities in human health and biomedicine.

9. **Service, Support, and Operations:** Each service and component within the GUARDIANS ecosystem will be supported by comprehensive user support, training, documentation, and robust service-level agreements. Continuous performance monitoring and improvement processes will be implemented to ensure reliable, efficient, and user-friendly operations. Technical and policy frameworks will be established to enable access to GUARDIANS services.

10. **Security:** Protecting sensitive human data is paramount. GUARDIANS infrastructure will implement industry-standard security measures, including an uplift to identity assurance, secure and shared authentication and authorisation control mechanisms based on best practice international architecture standards, together with auditing processes, and adherence to relevant data privacy regulations and best practices. Data security protocols will be regularly reviewed and updated to address emerging threats and maintain the highest levels of GUARDIANS user identity assurance and donor data protection and privacy.

# Appendix: Foundations and Inspirations

The GUARDIANS Guidelines are inspired by the fundamental principles of overarching organisations, including funding bodies and national and international omics research collaborations. These principles and foundations are outlined below.

## NCRIS principles

NRI comprises the nationally significant assets, facilities, services and associated expertise to support leading-edge research and innovation. It is accessible to publicly and privately funded users across Australia and internationally. NCRIS is the ongoing funding strategy underlying NRI. It’s objectives are:

- to catalyse and support a **national network** of research infrastructure projects
- through this network, support **high quality** research that will **drive greater innovation** in the Australian research sector and the economy more broadly
- offer **seamless access** to National Research Infrastructure (NRI) to empower Australian researchers to address key national and global challenges
- to **support collaboration** between **researchers** and other end users, such as **industry** both nationally and internationally

## Bioplatforms Australia principles

Bioplatforms Australia’s core mission is to facilitate life sciences research across environment, agriculture, health and industrial applications by developing, deploying and providing access to advanced omics, bioinformatics and biological collections infrastructure, platforms and expertise in a collaborative manner with the research community. Key principles emphasise:

- national collaboration, especially through peer-reviewed centres
- building on existing research programs
- international links
- appropriate technology deployment
- bioinformatics analysis support
- industry engagement, and
- requiring co-investment to maximise value and impact

## NAGIM principles

NAGIM (National Approach to Genomic Information Management) aims to develop a nationally coordinated and consistent approach to managing human genomic information across Australia. NAGIM principles cover critical technical capabilities, architectural approaches, fundamental ethical/legal tenets, as well as other key aspects necessary for a nationally unified genomic data ecosystem:  
Critical Infrastructure Pillars:

- Interoperability: Systems must be able to interact and seamlessly exchange information
- Scalability: Able to handle growth and process large-scale datasets
- Extensibility: Accommodating new additions and expanded functionality
- Usability: Ease of use meeting the needs of all end-users in research/clinical settings
- Security: Protecting data, infrastructure and applications from threats or misuse
- Sustainability: Ensuring long-term operational, financial viability and continuity

Architectural Enablers:

- Federated Frameworks: Interconnected yet autonomous systems
- Interoperability: Seamless interaction between systems
- Standards-based: Adhering to established data/technical standards
- Cloud/Hybrid Solutions: Leveraging cloud and on-premise infrastructure
- International Alignment: Conforming to global best practices

Fundamental Principles:

- Trust, Transparency, Accountability: Engendering confidence through openness
- Equity of Access: Fair and equitable access for all
- Indigenous Data Sovereignty: Respecting Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander control
- Regulatory/Legal/Cultural Alignment: Complying with Australian context
- Data Governance: Robust policies for data management
- Ethical/Social Considerations: Addressing ethical, legal and societal implications

Other Critical Aspects:

- Reliability: Delivering consistent and dependable performance
- Privacy Protection: Safeguarding sensitive personal information
- Technical/Governance Feasibility: Practical implementability
- Robustness: Resilient to errors, failures or attacks
- Auditability: Enabling monitoring and review of processes
- Fitness for Purpose: Meeting intended use case requirements
- Agility: Adaptable to evolving needs and technologies
- Distributable: Enabling decentralised deployment
- Affordability: Cost-effective solutions within budget constraints

**Additionally, the Blueprint for National Genomic Info Management lists these principles:**

- Consumer: Person-centred focus, trust, informed consent, right to access data, data portability, equity of access, benefit from use.
- Indigenous: Collective/individual benefit, authority to control data, responsibility to communities, engagement for ethics.
- Genomic Research: Rationalized repositories, ethical data provenance, reuse with permission, plan for change/scalability.
- Translation: Efficacy/utility/effectiveness, leverage research flexibility, emerging validated knowledge.
- Genomic Medicine: Maximise clinical benefit, support data sharing within/across healthcare settings, sustainable, frequently updated knowledge, genomic data as clinical data.
- Data Management: Clinical vs research data, multiple repositories, genomic data retention, national/international collaboration, governance models, contributing to/using references, pragmatism, data quality.

# BioCommons principles

The Australian BioCommons is a national digital infrastructure that supports life sciences research by providing a collaborative platform for data storage, analysis, and sharing. Supported by the Australian Government's NCRIS and hosted by Bioplatforms Australia and the University of Melbourne, the BioCommons aims to accelerate discoveries in areas like genomics, precision medicine, and biotechnology by enabling researchers to access and utilise vast biological datasets and advanced bioinformatics tools.

Key principles include:

- Align our activities with the vision and mission of Bioplatforms Australia and actively support its scientific initiatives and collaborations.
- Observe a national focus on capabilities and communities.
- Partner internationally: participate in and contribute to larger critical mass efforts where possible; reuse and improve rather than build anew.
- Partner locally: magnify our impact through the participation of institutional bioinformatics centres of expertise and national and institutional digital infrastructure providers.
- Build a software and expertise capability that will reduce duplication of infrastructure management in Australia and allow efforts to be re-focussed on methods development and dissemination.
- Promote the development of, and build on, high throughput cloud infrastructure that is interoperable with international (initially US and European) equivalents, using established, well supported software platforms.
- Streamline the exchange of tools, workflows, data and training and expertise both nationally and internationally.
- Develop and empower the talents and aspirations of our staff, our collaborators and our users.

## FAIR Principles

- To be Findable: (Meta)data assigned globally unique persistent identifiers, rich metadata, metadata includes data identifiers, (meta)data registered/indexed.
- To be Accessible: (Meta)data retrievable by identifiers via open protocol, metadata accessible even if data is no longer available.
- To be Interoperable: (Meta)data uses formal knowledge representation language, FAIR vocabularies, includes qualified references.
- To be Reusable: Rich (meta)data description, clear usage licences, detailed provenance, meeting community standards.

## CARE Principles

- Collective Benefit: For inclusive development, improved governance, equitable outcomes.
- Authority to Control: Recognizing rights/interests, data for governance, governance of data.
- Responsibility: For positive relationships, expanding capability/capacity, indigenous languages/worldviews.
- Ethics: Minimising harm/maximising benefit, justice, future use.
